III-06 / Components Modal Dialog.

A modal presents content in a dedicated mode that prevents interaction with the parent view and resolves through an explicit action. The component documented here covers three centered shapes — dialogue, form, and scrollable — all rendered on the system's glass surface. The edge-anchored blocking surface (drawer) lives alongside Side Panel on III-07.

Architectural note · scope boundaries

Modal is the centered blocking surface. A non-blocking, in-flow message is an Alert. A transient, auto-dismissing message is a Toast. A contextual surface anchored to a trigger (without a scrim, without focus trap) is a Dropdown or Popover. An edge-anchored blocking surface — focus-trapping, scroll-locking, dismissed through Esc, backdrop, or the close button — is a Drawer, documented on III-07 alongside Side Panel. Modal here is reserved for surfaces that must interrupt the underlying view from the centre: destructive confirmations, focused tasks, and content that needs a focus boundary to be usable.

Architectural note · one modal at a time

Stacked modals are not part of this system. Opening a second dialog over a first nests two focus traps and creates a context the user cannot reason about. When a flow needs more than one step, sequence the steps inside one dialog (multi-step) or promote the flow to a dedicated route. The single exception is a Critical alert dialog raised from inside another dialog to confirm a destructive action on its own contents — handled by replacing, not layering, the surface.

01 / Modal — Anatomy

Anatomy.

A modal is a Card with an envelope around it. The interior — the slot structure, the eyebrow + title + description + action-row patterns, and the glass surface itself — is the standard Card pattern from III-05. What makes this a modal is the envelope: the backdrop scrim that interrupts the parent view, the viewport-edge constraints that keep the surface readable, the size cap that forces the body to scroll when content overflows, and the dismiss contract that ties focus capture, scroll lock, and the three exit verbs together. The anatomy below documents the envelope. For the interior, see Card.

Title is the only required slot. A modal without a title cannot be referenced by assistive tech (aria-labelledby resolves to the title element) and has no docking handle for the user's attention. Description, body, and footer are optional — a one-line confirmation can omit body entirely and let the title carry the question.

The dismiss button is the explicit close affordance. Backdrop click and Escape are conveniences and may be suppressed for high-stakes confirmations (data-modal-static); the visible close button is not. Removing the close button traps the user inside a flow they cannot exit and is incorrect for any non-blocking dialog.

What gets shared?
.modal
The backdrop. Owns the scrim colour, the blur tier, the click-to-dismiss target, and the entry/exit fade. Fixed-position; dismissal toggles .is-open on this element.
.modal__surface
The envelope around the card. Owns max-width per size token (--modal-{xs|sm|md|lg|xl}-width), the max-height cap (--modal-max-height: min(86vh, 720px)) that forces the body to scroll on overflow, the viewport-edge gutters, the centred anchor, and the slide+scale entry transform.
.modal__close
The dismiss affordance. Always last in header DOM order, always present — the close button is the explicit exit verb. Esc and backdrop are implicit conveniences and may be suppressed for high-stakes confirmations (data-modal-static); the close button is not.

Interior — from Card (III-05). The slot structure inside the envelope follows Card's pattern; modal scopes its own BEM names (.modal__*) so it can carry its own padding and typography tokens without affecting Card. Slot reference below.

02 / Modal — Dialogue

Dialogue.

The envelope trims to header + footer — no body slot. The title carries the question, the optional description states the consequence, the footer carries the two actions. Used when the user must answer before the flow continues: a reassignment, a sign-off, a destructive confirmation.

The question is in the title, not the description. The user must be able to answer from the title alone. The description confirms what will be affected, not whether to proceed.

The primary action sits last. Reading order ends on the affirmative path. Cancel takes the secondary slot. Destructive primaries use .btn--caution; the surface itself stays neutral.

03 / Modal — Form

Form.

The envelope is the full vertical: header + body + footer. The body hosts a small, self-contained data-entry task — a quick capture, an inline edit, a single-step record. Surfaces longer than ~6 fields belong on a dedicated route, not in a modal.

A modal is the wrong place for a long form. Once the body needs to scroll past two viewport heights the user loses the spatial cue of the surrounding page, the focus trap becomes punitive, and field-level errors break the user's mental model of what's visible. Promote the flow to a dedicated route once the field count or required reading exceeds a single glance.

Submit affordance reflects state. The primary action is disabled until the form is valid, enters a loading state during submission (.btn--loading), and the close button is suppressed while the request is in flight so the user cannot dismiss the dialog mid-write.

04 / Modal — Scrollable

Scrollable.

The envelope pins the header and footer; the body scrolls inside the surface's height cap (--modal-max-height). Reserved for content the user must navigate top-to-bottom in a single sitting — terms, change-log, audit trail — where losing the dismiss control on scroll is incorrect. The seam between sticky slots and scrolling content is drawn by a hairline divider so the slot boundaries stay legible.

Scrolling content does not replace pagination. Lists with more than ~50 rows belong on a dedicated route with a real list component. The scrollable modal is for content that reads top-to-bottom in a single sitting (terms, audit trail, release notes) — not for lists that need filtering or sorting.

Records-retention policy

Terms of access · v3.2

Records older than seven years are read-only by default. Edit access can be re-enabled by an administrator on a per-record basis, and every edit on a re-enabled record is permanently logged in the audit trail.

Records flagged as part of an active compliance review are locked for the duration of the review window. Lock status is visible at the top of every chart and on the records index.

Sharing a chart externally generates a read-only access link valid for 24 hours. The link is single-use; a new link must be generated for every additional reviewer. The recipient cannot extend or forward the link.

External reviewers see the bloodwork panel, the patient's weight history, and any flagged withdrawal periods. No owner-identifying fields are shared. Sharing events are logged on the chart's activity timeline.

This policy supersedes versions 3.0 and 3.1. The previous policy text remains accessible in the change-log and is referenced from any audit-trail entry that pre-dates this revision.
